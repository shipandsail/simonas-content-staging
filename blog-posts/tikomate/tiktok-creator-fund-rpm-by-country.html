TikTok Creator Fund RPM by Country (2026): What Creators Actually Earn

TL;DR

  • The well-documented general pattern: Tier-1 English-speaking markets (US, UK, Canada, Australia) pay meaningfully more per 1,000 views than most other regions, because advertiser demand and ad rates are higher there.
  • This is the core reason international creators try to build a genuine US-viewing audience, and why a US-registered account with real US engagement history matters for monetization, not just for eligibility.

Ask any group of TikTok creators about Creator Fund or Creator Rewards RPM and you'll get wildly different numbers. That's because RPM (revenue per 1,000 views) is not a fixed rate, it moves based on audience geography, niche, video length, and advertiser demand at any given time, and TikTok has never published an official country-by-country rate card. What is well established, and repeated consistently by creators and industry observers, is the general pattern: some markets pay noticeably more than others, and the gap is large enough to change a creator's entire monetization strategy. Here's what's actually known, what isn't, and why so many international creators end up chasing the US audience specifically.

Why does TikTok Creator Fund RPM vary by country?

TikTok's Creator Rewards Program (which replaced the original Creator Fund in 2023) pays creators based on video views, but the underlying economics are tied to advertising revenue. TikTok earns more per impression from advertisers targeting audiences in markets with higher ad spend, and a portion of that revenue flows back to creators through the rewards program. Markets where advertisers pay more per ad impression tend to translate into higher creator RPM, this is the same basic dynamic seen across other ad-supported platforms, not something unique to TikTok.

  • Advertiser demand drives the pool: Regions with more competitive ad markets generally support higher creator payouts.
  • Niche still matters within a country: Even inside a high-paying market, some content categories (finance, business, education) tend to attract more advertiser interest than others (pure entertainment, memes).
  • TikTok doesn't publish exact figures: Unlike a transparent ad-rate card, Creator Rewards RPM is not disclosed as a fixed number per country, creators only see their own account's actual performance in Creator Center.

Which countries tend to pay the most per 1,000 views?

Without an official published rate card, the safest way to describe this is qualitatively: Tier-1 English-speaking markets, most notably the United States, alongside the UK, Canada, and Australia, are widely and consistently described by creators and industry observers as paying meaningfully higher RPM than most other regions. This tracks with what's known about digital advertising more broadly, where these markets command some of the highest ad rates in the world.

Beyond that general pattern, we won't cite specific cents-per-view numbers for individual countries here, TikTok has not published verified figures, and any precise number circulating online should be treated as an unverified estimate rather than fact.

⚠
Be skeptical of exact RPM numbers you see online
TikTok has never published an official per-country or per-niche RPM rate card for the Creator Rewards Program. Any specific dollar or cent figure you see quoted for "average RPM in [country]" is a creator-reported estimate, not a disclosed platform rate. Your actual RPM depends on your specific audience, niche, and content, check your own Creator Center for the real number.

Why do so many international creators try to build a US audience?

Because the general pattern above is consistent enough that it shapes strategy: if Tier-1 markets like the US pay meaningfully more per view, then a creator's total earnings depend heavily on where their actual viewers are located, not just where the creator happens to live. A creator based outside the US whose content is watched primarily by a non-US audience is, by the same general pattern, likely earning at a lower rate than a creator whose audience is concentrated in the US, all else being equal.

This is compounded by the fact that TikTok's algorithm defaults new accounts to an audience matching their registered location and early engagement history. An account registered outside the US, without deliberate effort, tends to accumulate a non-US audience, which, per the general RPM pattern, caps its earning potential relative to an account that builds a genuine US-viewing base.
